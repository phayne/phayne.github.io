Moon

polar volatiles and surface thermal properties

The Moon is a primary focus of our research, particularly understanding the distribution, stability, and accessibility of water ice and other volatiles in permanently shadowed regions near the lunar poles.

(Left) Shackleton crater's rim at the Moon's south pole. (Middle) Permanently shadowed regions that may harbor water ice. (Right) Small-scale "micro cold traps" greatly expand the potential reservoir for lunar ice.

Polar Volatiles

Water ice and other volatiles are trapped in permanently shadowed regions (PSRs) near the lunar poles, where temperatures can drop below 40 K (Paige et al. 2010). Our group uses thermal models and remote sensing data to map these cold traps and understand the processes that control volatile delivery, retention, and loss, including new observational constraints on the long-term history of polar ice accumulation (Aharonson et al. 2026).

Surface Thermophysical Properties

We use data from the Diviner Lunar Radiometer Experiment aboard the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter to characterize the thermal properties of the lunar regolith (Hayne et al. 2017). These measurements reveal the structure and composition of the upper few centimeters of the surface, with implications for landing site selection and resource utilization. Recent work has extended the thermophysical framework with microphysical models of regolith porosity and latitudinal structure (Bürger et al. 2024).

Micro Cold Traps

Our research has shown that water ice may be trapped in small-scale cold traps—shadows cast by rocks and small craters—that are far more numerous than the large permanently shadowed regions (Hayne et al. 2021). These micro cold traps could significantly increase the accessible water inventory on the Moon, and their stability depends on local self‑heating and surface roughness (Formisano et al. 2025).

Artemis II Flyby Validation

During the Artemis II lunar flyby, the crew captured high-resolution photographs of the Moon that we used to validate PTM3D, our 3D ray-traced illumination and thermophysical model. See the blog post for details.

Artemis II astronaut photograph (left) and PTM3D-rendered synthetic image (right) for the same camera pose, solar geometry, and Nikon D5 + 50 mm optics.
